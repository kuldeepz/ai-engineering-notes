Foundation

Why AI roadmap prioritisation is different

Standard product prioritisation frameworks break down for AI initiatives. The inputs are uncertain, the outcomes are probabilistic, and the infrastructure cost is non-trivial before you build the first feature.

🗃️

Data dependency

You cannot build the model without first solving data availability, quality, and labelling. This upstream work is often invisible in standard product planning.

Data-first planning
🎲

Outcome uncertainty

ML outcomes are probabilistic. A model might reach 85% accuracy or 60%. Standard product roadmaps assume deterministic delivery — AI ones cannot.

Use ranges, not points
💸

Infrastructure cost

AI requires compute, storage, and tooling investment before a single user feature ships. This "tax" must be explicit in every AI roadmap and business case.

Price in infra cost

Key insight: ROI for AI is harder to predict than for standard software. Use confidence intervals — "this initiative could reduce cost by £80k–£300k depending on model performance" — not single-point estimates. This is not hedging; it is honest communication.

Initiative Types

Four types of AI initiatives — click each

Every AI initiative fits into one of four categories. Each has a different risk profile, timeline, and prioritisation logic.

⚡

Quick Wins

+

Rule-based automation, simple classifiers

under 4 weeks

Characteristics: High certainty, narrow scope, usually rule-based or simple ML. Data is already clean and available.

When to prioritise: Early in the AI journey to build credibility and demonstrate ROI. Also useful when stakeholder trust is low.

Risk level: Low — outcome is largely predictable before you start.

Measure success by: Adoption rate, time saved, error rate reduction. Should be measurable within 2 weeks of launch.

🧠

Core ML Features

+

Classification, recommendation, ranking

1–3 months

Characteristics: Standard ML problems with established approaches. Data work is the main variable. Model architecture is known.

When to prioritise: When you have clean labelled data and a well-defined business problem with measurable outcome.

Risk level: Medium — data quality and labelling time are the main unknowns.

Measure success by: Offline metrics (accuracy, F1, NDCG) and online business metrics (CTR, conversion, revenue).

🏗️

Platform Investments

+

MLOps, feature store, model registry

3–6 months

Characteristics: Foundational infrastructure that enables faster future delivery. No direct user-facing value but multiplies team output.

When to prioritise: When teams are duplicating data pipeline or serving work, or when MLOps is a manual bottleneck.

Risk level: Low technical risk, high organisational risk — hard to get funding for invisible infrastructure.

Measure success by: Time to deploy a new model, experiment velocity, incident frequency.

🚀

Moonshots

+

Generative AI, agents, novel capabilities

6+ months

Characteristics: High uncertainty on both technical feasibility and business impact. May require new data sources, new infrastructure, and new evaluation frameworks.

When to prioritise: When the potential upside justifies the risk and the organisation has enough ML maturity to de-risk effectively.

Risk level: High — timelines and outcomes are highly uncertain. Treat as research investment, not product roadmap item.

Measure success by: Prototype quality gates, not production shipping dates. Set stage gates: proof of concept, pilot, production.

Build vs Buy

When to build vs buy AI capabilities

The wrong decision here wastes months. Build when it is your core differentiator. Buy or use open source when it is a commodity.

Build

Core differentiator

When the ML capability is what makes your product unique and defensible. Building creates proprietary data flywheels and IP. Examples: TikTok's recommendation system, Stripe's fraud model.

If it's your moat
Buy / Use APIs

Commodity capabilities

Sentiment analysis, translation, OCR, speech-to-text — these are solved problems. Use Azure AI, OpenAI, Google AI, or AWS AI services. Don't rebuild what is already solved.

If it's commodity
Open Source

When flexibility matters

Use open-source models (Llama, Mistral, HuggingFace) when you need customisation, data privacy, or cost control, but don't need to train from scratch. Gives control without full build cost.

Control + speed
Partner

Domain expertise needed

For highly specialised domains (medical imaging, legal NLP, financial risk) where a specialist vendor has years of labelled data and domain knowledge you cannot replicate quickly.

Domain is the moat
Stakeholder Alignment

Getting buy-in for AI investment

Different stakeholders need to hear different things. The technical truth stays the same — the framing must fit the audience and their incentives.

💰

Finance / CFO

Lead with cost reduction, revenue impact, and risk mitigation. Use ranges. Show payback period. Separate infrastructure investment from model development cost. Include compute and re-training in TCO.

⚙️

Engineering leadership

Lead with technical feasibility, data availability, and infrastructure readiness. Show you have a plan for MLOps, monitoring, and failure handling. Acknowledge uncertainty honestly.

🎯

Product leadership

Lead with user value and timeline to first value. Show how AI improves a metric they already care about. Separate the research phase from the product shipping phase clearly.

⚖️

Legal / Compliance

Lead with risk guardrails, explainability, and audit trails. Show bias testing plans, data governance, and EU AI Act or GDPR compliance considerations. Never surprise legal with AI in production.

Quarterly Planning

How to structure an AI quarterly roadmap

Divide capacity across three buckets every quarter: platform (infrastructure that enables the team), features (user-facing AI), and experiments (high-uncertainty research). Balance matters.

Q1 — Foundation
PLATFORM 40%
Feature store, model registry setup
FEATURES 40%
1 core ML feature end-to-end
EXPERIMENTS 20%
Validate one moonshot hypothesis
Q2 — Deliver
PLATFORM 25%
MLOps automation, monitoring
FEATURES 55%
2–3 ML features in production
EXPERIMENTS 20%
Prototype from Q1 experiment
Q3 — Scale
PLATFORM 20%
Drift monitoring, retraining pipelines
FEATURES 60%
Scale winning features, iterate
EXPERIMENTS 20%
Next quarter moonshot validation
Q4 — Invest
PLATFORM 30%
Next-year infra investment
FEATURES 40%
Consolidate, reduce model debt
EXPERIMENTS 30%
Moonshot ramp-up for next year
📌

Never drop below 20% platform

Platform investment below 20% means the team accumulates infrastructure debt that blocks delivery in the following quarter.

🔬

Always protect experiment budget

Experiments are how you generate next year's roadmap. Cutting them means shipping the same ideas forever — competitors who experiment overtake you.

⚖️

Re-estimate at each quarter gate

AI timelines shift as you learn. Re-score initiatives at the start of each quarter with updated information — don't hold to a plan made without data.

Common Pitfalls

Prioritisation mistakes that waste AI investment

These four mistakes are the most common causes of AI roadmaps that fail to deliver value on time or at all.

❌

Over-investing in moonshots

Dedicating more than 30% of capacity to high-uncertainty moonshots with no quick wins means 6 months pass with nothing to show stakeholders.

Credibility risk
❌

Ignoring data quality

Prioritising model work before confirming data quality means discovering fundamental data problems mid-project when the timeline is already committed.

Sunk cost risk
❌

No success metrics upfront

Starting an AI initiative without defining what "done" looks like means stakeholders can always move the goalposts and the project never officially succeeds.

Governance risk
❌

Underestimating infra cost

The business case includes model development cost but not training compute, serving infrastructure, monitoring tooling, or data labelling costs. Budgets blow out.

Budget risk
Prioritisation Walkthrough

From initiative list to prioritised roadmap

A step-by-step process for converting a list of AI ideas into a sequenced, defensible quarterly roadmap.

Step 1 — Collect and categorise initiatives: Start with a raw list of AI ideas from across the organisation. Categorise each as: quick win, core ML feature, platform investment, or moonshot. Discard ideas with no clear business owner — they will never get traction.

Step 2 — Score each initiative: Apply the 5-dimension scoring framework (business impact, data availability, technical feasibility, time to value, strategic alignment). Use the weighted formula. Involve one data scientist and one product stakeholder in scoring to reduce bias.

Step 3 — Apply the build vs buy filter: For every initiative, ask whether this is a core differentiator or a commodity. Replace commodity work with vendor APIs or open-source models. This can cut the build list by 30–50%.

Step 4 — Sequence by data readiness: Sort top-scored initiatives by data availability. Initiatives with clean, available data ship first. Initiatives requiring labelling, data acquisition, or governance approval are pushed to a later quarter, and data work begins in advance.

Step 5 — Allocate to quarters using the 40/40/20 rule: Allocate platform work (40% in Q1, dropping to 20% by Q3), feature work (40–60%), and experiments (20–30%) each quarter. Ensure at least one quick win ships in Q1.

North star principle: Every item on the AI roadmap must connect directly to a business metric. If you cannot complete the sentence "this initiative will move [metric] by [range] because [mechanism]", it is not ready to be on the roadmap.
