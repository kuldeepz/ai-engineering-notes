Foundation

Why AI teams fail without the right structure

Most AI team failures are not technical — they are organisational. The wrong structure creates bottlenecks, misaligned incentives, and projects that never reach production.

🔬

Data scientists without engineers

Models live in notebooks. Without software engineers and MLOps, there is no path to production. Proof of concepts never become products.

Models Never Ship
⚙️

Engineers without data scientists

Software ships but ML quality is poor. Engineers optimise for code quality, not model performance. No one owns prediction accuracy or reliability.

No ML Quality
🏗️

No platform team

Every team rebuilds feature pipelines, model registries, and serving infrastructure from scratch. 80% of effort goes to plumbing instead of value delivery.

Duplicated Effort
Core Roles

8 roles every mature AI team needs — click each

Each role fills a distinct gap. Missing any one creates a systematic bottleneck that slows every project across the organisation.

🤖

ML Engineer

+

Day to day: Training, evaluating, and iterating on models; writing production feature engineering code; driving models from notebook to serving.

Must-have skills: Python, PyTorch/TensorFlow, feature engineering, distributed training, model serving APIs.

Hiring mistake: Hiring only researchers — you need engineers who write production-grade code, not just notebooks.

📊

Data Scientist

+

Day to day: Exploratory analysis, hypothesis formation, experiment design, model selection, and communicating findings to stakeholders.

Must-have skills: Statistics, Python/R, SQL, A/B experiment design, data storytelling.

Hiring mistake: Expecting them to deploy — data scientists need ML engineers to productionise their work.

📱

AI Product Manager

+

Day to day: Translating business problems to ML problems, defining success metrics, managing expectations around probabilistic outputs.

Must-have skills: ML fundamentals, stakeholder management, metric definition, data literacy.

Hiring mistake: Hiring a regular PM — they must understand ML uncertainty and how to evaluate models.

🔧

ML Platform Engineer

+

Day to day: Building and maintaining feature stores, model registries, experiment tracking, training pipelines, and serving infrastructure.

Must-have skills: Kubernetes, distributed systems, CI/CD, Spark, Kubeflow or similar, cloud platforms.

Hiring mistake: Hiring too late — platform debt compounds and slows every team member.

🗄️

Data Engineer

+

Day to day: Building data pipelines, ensuring data quality, managing data warehouses, and creating feature pipelines for training.

Must-have skills: SQL, Spark, dbt, Airflow/Prefect, data modelling, Kafka for streaming.

Hiring mistake: Treating as optional — without clean reliable data, no ML project succeeds.

🛡️

AI Safety / Ethics

+

Day to day: Bias auditing, fairness testing, red teaming, policy compliance reviews, adversarial testing of AI systems.

Must-have skills: Fairness metrics, adversarial ML, regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, GDPR), red teaming.

Hiring mistake: Adding as an afterthought — safety must be embedded from the start, not bolted on.

🚀

MLOps Engineer

+

Day to day: CI/CD for ML pipelines, model monitoring, drift detection, A/B testing infrastructure, retraining automation.

Must-have skills: MLflow/W&B, Prometheus/Grafana, Docker/Kubernetes, Python, GitHub Actions.

Hiring mistake: Confusing with DevOps — MLOps requires understanding model behaviour, not just infrastructure.

👔

AI Engineering Manager

+

Day to day: Technical roadmapping, team building, cross-functional stakeholder management, unblocking engineers, managing ML uncertainty.

Must-have skills: ML project management, hiring, technical depth to review ML designs, stakeholder communication.

Hiring mistake: Promoting the best data scientist — management needs entirely different skills from technical excellence.

Maturity Stages

Three stages of AI team evolution

AI teams grow through predictable stages. Each stage has different priorities, key hires, and success metrics. Trying to skip stages is the most common mistake.

01

Experimenting

2–5 people. Proving AI can create tangible business value. Most work is prototype-grade.

Key hires: 1 ML Engineer, 1 Data Scientist

Challenge: Data access and stakeholder buy-in

Success metric: At least one model serving real traffic

🔬 Prototype-first📊 Prove ROI
02

Scaling

10–20 people. Platform building. Multiple models in production across several teams.

Key hires: ML Platform Eng., MLOps, Data Engineers

Challenge: Tech debt and duplicated infrastructure

Success metric: Models retrain automatically; drift is monitored

🏗️ Platform-first⚡ Reduce friction
03

Mature

50+ people. AI-first culture. ML is embedded across every product and product decision.

Key hires: AI Safety, Governance, Specialised researchers

Challenge: Governance, safety, and org alignment at scale

Success metric: AI contributes measurably to core business OKRs

🛡️ Safety🌐 AI-native culture

Red flags when hiring

Cannot explain model tradeoffs. No production experience. Treats Kaggle leaderboard performance as production quality. Shows no interest in data quality.

✅

What good looks like

Talks about failure modes before results. Has shipped something to production. Asks about data before the model. Understands evaluation beyond accuracy.

🎯

How to assess ML candidates

Give a real dataset with data quality issues. Ask them to design an evaluation framework. Ask what went wrong on a past project and how they fixed it.

Common Mistakes

Structural mistakes that kill AI teams

These four patterns appear repeatedly in organisations that struggle to scale AI beyond prototypes.

❌

Hiring only data scientists

Without ML engineers, data engineers, and MLOps, data scientists become permanently blocked and models never reach production.

Critical
❌

No MLOps from day 1

Manual deployments and absent monitoring creates fragile systems. Retraining takes weeks. Model degradation goes undetected in production.

Critical
❌

Ignoring data engineering

Data scientists spend 80% of time cleaning data because no one owns pipelines. Every new project restarts the same data wrangling from scratch.

Structural
❌

No product alignment

The AI team builds impressive models that solve the wrong problems. Without an AI PM, there is no bridge between user needs and ML capabilities.

Team Design Guide

Building an AI team from 0 to 50

A practical walkthrough for scaling an AI organisation through all three maturity stages, with clear guidance on sequencing hires.

Hires 1–3 (Stage 1 — Prove value): Start with one strong ML Engineer who can also do data science work. Add a Data Engineer as soon as data pipelines become painful and repetitive. Validate one business use case end-to-end before adding headcount.

Hires 4–10 (Transition to Stage 2 — Build platform): Hire an ML Platform Engineer before the team grows further — platform debt compounds fast. Add an AI Product Manager to manage stakeholder expectations and shape the roadmap. Bring in a second ML Engineer and a dedicated Data Scientist.

Hires 11–25 (Scale — Embed and specialise): Begin embedding ML Engineers in product teams using a hub-and-spoke model. Hire an MLOps Engineer to automate retraining and monitoring. Build a feature store and model registry to eliminate duplication.

Hires 26–50 (Mature — Governance and safety): Add an AI Safety / Ethics role once models are in high-stakes decisions affecting users. Hire an AI Engineering Manager with experience leading ML teams at scale. Consider specialised researchers for differentiated capabilities that create defensible IP.

Key principle: Always hire platform and data engineering capabilities earlier than feels necessary. Under-investing in infrastructure compounds in cost at every subsequent headcount milestone.
